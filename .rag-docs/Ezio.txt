DOCUMENT METADATA

title: Concrete Disney: The Obscene Avant-Garde
author: Ezio Yitang Zhang
date: 2026-02-21
type: architectural theory essay
keywords: disneyization, avant-garde, tectonics, commodification, semiotics, brutalism, capital, materiality, architecture critique

SECTION 1

Luna Park is the first manifestation of a curse that is to haunt the architectural profession for the rest of its life, the formula: technology + cardboard (or any other flimsy material) = reality.1

SECTION 2

The greatest crisis facing contemporary architectural practice is not the collapse of faith or critique, but the complete decoding, abstraction, and commodification of architectural sign production by capital, what may be more precisely described as the systemic Disneyization2 of tectonic language. Koolhaas’s diagnosis of Manhattan’s delirium thus reads less as celebration than as prophecy: whether in aggressive sans-serif typography or rough-cast concrete, signs once charged with social antagonism and tectonic honesty have been neutralized into sterile capital attire.

SECTION 3

As Frederic Thompson boasted of Luna Park, there was satisfaction in the “brilliant application” of architectural principles (endlessly and madly stacking spiky towers) successfully exploiting the subconscious anxiety of escaping Manhattan’s grid.3

Today, “Concrete Disney” manipulates a different pathology: the collective psychosis of subjects who, in the deluge of digital images, have lost not merely gravity but any stable relation to material resistance and embodied tactility.

SECTION 4

From the concrete apartment in Tár4, hyper-decoded by social media frenzy, to the Khaite flagship store in New York, where preserved construction “imperfections” theatrically contrast with cashmere commodities; from Rick Owens’s brutalist runway in Paris, staged as absolute danger yet structurally overdetermined by safety codes5, to university students in Sydney pouring hollow nostalgia over Yugoslav Spomeniks via TikTok.

SECTION 5

The final strangulation of the avant-garde, as Manfredo Tafuri argued, is not destruction but neutralization: to render it “harmless,” to strip it of its structural rupture with reality and reduce it to an aesthetic backdrop.

Balenciaga’s 2023 Hamburg spatial operation exemplifies this mechanism. Within its proclaimed “atemporal” setting, gray velvet curtains are chemically washed to simulate duration, and aluminum tables are disguised as outdoor debris through costly pigmentation.6

Yet behind this expensive concrete façade lies a vulgar typology: a grid of shelves and cash registers indistinguishable from the H&M next door. Behind the staged imperfection operates an extremely costly concealed light steel frame, performing tirelessly. This is not danger but castrated simulation, a transformation of post-war scarcity into bourgeois absolute safety, a hygienic appropriation rather than true Spolia Opima.

SECTION 6

In this arena of meaninglessness, it becomes necessary to return to Reyner Banham’s original articulation of New Brutalism.7 It was not a visual style but an ethic: an ethic of legibility, of material truthfulness, of categorical refusal of structural disguise.

When architecture betrays this ethic for the sake of image, “What you see is what you get” ceases to guarantee technical integrity and instead marks the complete flattening of ontological depth.

Architecture today must rediscover the objecthood and resistance of materials. Otherwise, what remains is an endless illusion of ruin, sustained not by structural necessity but by air conditioning and adhesive.
